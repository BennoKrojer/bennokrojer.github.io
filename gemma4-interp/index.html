LatentLens on Gemma4-12B: Inspecting Visual and Audio Token Representations

Benno Krojer — June 2026

Gemma4-12B was released by Google a few weeks ago and it piqued my interest as an interp researcher since it diverges from the mainstream paradigm: it is an encoder-free vision-language model that processes image patches and audio segments directly as tokens, without a separate vision or audio encoder. This raises an interpretability question: what do those non-text tokens represent at each layer of the transformer?

We apply two complementary probes to every visual/audio token at every layer. LatentLens (Krojer et al., 2026; normalized) retrieves the nearest-neighbor tokens from a large caption corpus in the model's own representation space, after correcting for rogue dimensions via z-score standardization. I had developed this method recently (presenting at ICML in Seoul) and was curious how well it would work here. Interestingly, I had to add the normalization step to account for outlier dimensions that otherwise dominate cosine similarity. I also apply the more established LogitLens (nostalgebraist, 2020), which projects the raw hidden state through the unembedding matrix to return ranked vocabulary predictions. While LatentLens retrieves corpus tokens by similarity search, LogitLens reads off vocabulary logits directly; comparing both across layers reveals how the model processes non-text modalities.

Key insight

LatentLens (normalized) and LogitLens are complementary: LatentLens is the only informative probe in layers 8–28, while LogitLens takes over in layers 36–44. The rogue-dimension collapse at layer 16 — where all raw cosine similarities spike to >0.99 — makes LatentLens (unnormalized) completely uninformative at that layer. Normalizing by z-score standardization before retrieval corrects this and recovers strong signal.

Audio: Speech Tokens (LibriSpeech)

We probe 50 speech clips from LibriSpeech test-clean (3–15 seconds each, ~25 tokens/sec). Each 40ms audio token is inspected independently. We measure whether the top-5 predictions match words from the clip's transcript, using exact and stem matching after stopword removal.

LatentLens (normalized) peaks at 28.3% at layer 16, precisely where LogitLens collapses to 0% due to rogue dimensions — mirroring the vision finding. LogitLens recovers and overtakes LatentLens at layers 36–44 (27.4%). The random cross-clip baseline stays at ~2–3% throughout, confirming the signal is genuine.

Temporal structure

The two panels tell different stories. Absolute timing (left) is low: even at the peak layer (L16, 6.3%), most tokens do not predict the word being spoken at that exact moment. Ordering (right) is high: when predictions do match transcript words, they appear in near-perfect transcript sequence (Spearman ρ ≈ 0.96–1.00 at L16, ~0.93–0.96 at L36–44).

In other words: at layer 16, the model appears to have encoded the vocabulary and sequence of the entire utterance, but without precise temporal localization. Temporal precision develops later, as LogitLens becomes informative at L36+.

Try in demo Clip #1 (speech_00000, “CONCORD RETURNED TO ITS PLACE AMIDST THE TENTS”), Layer 16: LatentLens predicts “concord” → “returned” → “place” → “amidst” in order, all in the second half of the clip.

Audio: Environmental Sound Tokens (ESC-50)

We probe 50 environmental sound clips from ESC-50 (one per category, 5 seconds each). There is no transcript to match against; instead we measure semantic similarity between each top-5 prediction and the clip's category label (e.g. “crow”, “rain”) using sentence-transformer cosine similarity.

LatentLens (unnorm. and norm.) show a small but statistically significant advantage over the random baseline at layers 24–44 (t ≈ 4.8, surviving Bonferroni correction). The effect is modest in absolute terms (~0.04 cosine similarity difference); the predictions are not reliably interpretable as category labels by inspection. The most notable feature is the sharp dip at layer 16 for LatentLens (unnorm.), which drops below baseline — the same rogue-dimension collapse seen in vision and speech.

Summary

Three findings appear consistently across both modalities and both probe methods:

  1. Rogue dimensions cause a phase transition at layer 16. Raw cosine similarities spike to >0.99, collapsing LogitLens and LatentLens (unnorm.) outputs. Z-score normalization recovers the signal and reveals the strongest interpretable content at this layer.
  2. LatentLens and LogitLens are complementary. LatentLens (norm.) is informative in layers 8–28; LogitLens takes over in layers 36–44. In between is a transition zone where neither works well.
  3. Representations encode sequence before precise timing. For speech, predictions match transcript words in the correct temporal order (Spearman ρ ≈ 0.96) before they achieve precise timestamp alignment (6% exact timing at peak). A similar lag appears in vision, where the last-layer LogitLens predicts the next OCR word rather than the current one.
